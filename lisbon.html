🇵🇹 The Trip

The story

Hills, history and a city that has it all.

"Lisbon is one of those cities that surprises you — not because you didn't expect it to be good, but because it turns out to be even better than you hoped. Every corner has something worth stopping for."

We visited Lisbon in 2023 and it immediately jumped to the top of our European city break list. The city has a brilliantly lived-in feel — grand squares and monasteries sitting alongside tiny neighbourhood cafés, colourful tiled buildings and trams rattling up impossibly steep streets.

The hop-on hop-off bus was genuinely one of the best decisions we made. Given how hilly Lisbon is, it takes the hard work out of getting between the main sights and gives you a great overview of the city before you decide where to spend more time. Highly recommend it as a starting point.

Belém was a highlight — the Jerónimos Monastery is extraordinary, the Tower of Belém is iconic, and while you're there you are legally required to stop for a pastel de nata at the original Pastéis de Belém. Non-negotiable.

The gluten-free options in Lisbon were genuinely impressive too — Portuguese cuisine lends itself well to GF eating and we never struggled to find something brilliant on the menu.

And the day trips — Sintra, Colares and Cascais — added a completely different dimension to the trip. Each one felt like its own world, and all three are easy to reach from the city.

🏰 Sintra

The must-do day trip

Sintra — fairytale palaces and forested hillsides

If you are visiting Lisbon and you do not make it to Sintra, you are missing one of the most extraordinary places in all of Portugal. Just 40 minutes by train from the city, Sintra feels like stepping into a completely different world — colourful palaces perched on misty hilltops, lush gardens and winding cobbled streets full of pastry shops and palace views.

The Pena Palace is the iconic one — vivid, dramatic and unlike anything else you will have seen. But even just wandering the town itself and taking in the atmosphere is worth the trip out. Give yourself a full day here — you will want it.

🌊 Cascais

The lovely coastal town

Cascais — easy, breezy and well worth the trip

Cascais is one of those places that just feels right the moment you arrive. A short train ride along the coast from Lisbon, it has a completely different pace — relaxed, pretty and full of great seafood restaurants, lovely beaches and a charming old town centre that is perfect for an afternoon wander.

It works brilliantly as a half day or a full day out from Lisbon — you can even combine it with a stop in Estoril on the way. A lovely way to end a trip to the Lisbon area.

Common questions

Lisbon FAQ

Is Lisbon worth visiting?
Absolutely — Lisbon is one of the best city break destinations in Europe. It has stunning architecture, incredible food, a brilliant atmosphere and more to see than you can fit into one trip. The day trips alone to Sintra and Cascais make it worth it. We would go back in a heartbeat.
Is the hop-on hop-off bus worth it in Lisbon?
Yes — especially given how hilly Lisbon is. The hop-on hop-off bus is a brilliant way to cover a lot of ground without destroying your legs on the steep streets. It hits all the main sights and gives you a great overview of the city. We really recommend doing it on your first day to get your bearings.
Is Sintra worth a day trip from Lisbon?
Without question. Sintra is one of the most beautiful places in Portugal — fairytale palaces, lush hillside gardens and incredible views. It is only about 40 minutes from Lisbon by train and is an absolute must on any visit. Give yourself a full day and go early to beat the crowds.
Is Cascais worth visiting?
Cascais is a gorgeous coastal town just along the coast from Lisbon — easy to reach by train and well worth a half day or full day. It has a lovely relaxed atmosphere, great seafood, beautiful beaches and a very different feel to the busy city. A really lovely way to finish a Lisbon trip.
Is Lisbon good for gluten-free food?
Yes — Lisbon has a really good range of gluten-free options and awareness of dietary requirements is generally good in restaurants. Portuguese cuisine naturally includes a lot of fresh fish, grilled meats and rice dishes which tend to be naturally gluten-free. We found it one of the easier European cities for GF eating.
How many days do you need in Lisbon?
We would recommend at least four to five days to do Lisbon justice — that gives you time to explore the city properly, do the hop-on hop-off bus, visit Belém and its monuments, and still have time for day trips to Sintra and Cascais. You could easily fill a week without running out of things to do.
