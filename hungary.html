Budapest —
grand old Europe, thermal baths and ruin bars

We spent three nights in Budapest and it immediately became one of our favourite European city breaks. A city of extraordinary grandeur split in two by the Danube — the grand boulevards and energy of Pest on one side, the castle hill and quiet elegance of Buda on the other. The Parliament building lit up at night, Buda Castle glowing from the hill, the Chain Bridge, Fisherman's Bastion, the Gellért thermal baths, ruin bars that felt like nowhere else in Europe, food that was brilliant and cost almost nothing. Budapest in 2009 had a faded elegance that was completely intoxicating.

🌉 The Story

Faded elegance, cheap beer and one of Europe's great cities

"Budapest had an atmosphere unlike any Western European city we had visited. Grand Austro-Hungarian architecture on every street — enormous, ornate, slightly weathered — trams rattling past, thermal steam rising from grates, the smell of goulash from restaurant doorways and then, at night, the whole city glowing in the dark reflected in the Danube. It was completely intoxicating."

Budapest in 2009 was at a particular moment — Eastern Europe had joined the EU, cheap flights had opened it up to Irish and British weekend trippers, and the city had this extraordinary quality of being simultaneously grand and slightly rough around the edges. The communist era had left its marks on the outer districts but the centre was magnificent — Austro-Hungarian imperial architecture on a scale that rivalled Vienna, thermal baths that had been functioning for centuries, a river that split the city into two completely different personalities.

We stayed on the Pest side, which is where most visitors naturally gravitate — the flat, busy, commercial heart of the city with its grand boulevards, the famous ruin bars, the restaurants and the nightlife. Buda across the river felt quieter, more residential, more atmospheric at night — the castle on the hill above, the narrow streets below, the funicular creaking up the steep slope.

And the price of everything. In 2009, Budapest was almost absurdly affordable for Irish visitors. Enormous portions of goulash soup for almost nothing. Beer cheaper than at home by a factor of three or four. Dinner for two including wine for less than the price of a round of drinks in Dublin. The combination of extraordinary beauty, fascinating history and genuinely cheap living made it one of the best value city breaks in Europe.

🏛️ The Big Sights

Budapest's unmissable landmarks

Landmark 1
🏛️ Hungarian Parliament Building — the most beautiful in Europe

The Hungarian Parliament Building is simply one of the most extraordinary buildings in the world — a neo-Gothic masterpiece on the Pest bank of the Danube, 268 metres long, topped with a central dome and 96 towers, built between 1885 and 1904 and still the third-largest parliament building on earth. From across the river on the Buda side it is genuinely breath-taking — and at night, lit up and reflected in the Danube, it is one of the most beautiful urban views in all of Europe. Everyone photographs it. No photograph does it justice.

Landmark 2
🌉 Chain Bridge — the iconic crossing

The Széchenyi Chain Bridge is Budapest's most famous bridge and one of the most recognisable bridges in Europe — a beautiful suspension bridge connecting Pest to the foot of Castle Hill in Buda, opened in 1849 and rebuilt after the Second World War. Walking across it gives you the full sense of the Danube's width and the extraordinary views in both directions — the Parliament upstream, the castle hill behind you. At night, lit up, it is magnificent.

Landmark 3
🏰 Buda Castle & the Funicular

Buda Castle sits on a hill above the Danube and the historic Castle District around it is one of the most atmospheric areas in the city — cobbled streets, baroque townhouses, little restaurants and the sense of a medieval city that survived everything the 20th century threw at it. You can walk up via steep paths from the Chain Bridge end or take the historic Castle Hill Funicular — a short but very fun ride that deposits you at the top with views already opening up over Pest below.

Landmark 4
🏯 Fisherman's Bastion — the fairy-tale terrace

Fisherman's Bastion is one of those places that looks almost too pretty to be real — a neo-Romanesque terrace of white towers and walkways on the Buda hill, built at the turn of the 20th century as a decorative viewing platform. The views from it over the Danube, the Chain Bridge and the Parliament on the opposite bank are extraordinary — especially at sunrise or in the blue hour before dark. It is genuinely one of the most beautiful viewpoints in Europe.

Landmark 5
⛪ St Stephen's Basilica & the city streets

St Stephen's Basilica is the largest church in Budapest — a grand neoclassical building that took 54 years to complete and can hold 8,500 people. The dome offers brilliant views over central Pest. The streets around it — Andrássy Avenue, the grand boulevard that runs from the centre to Heroes' Square — are lined with extraordinary Austro-Hungarian architecture, now filled with restaurants, cafés and shops.

🌙 Budapest After Dark

The Danube at night — one of Europe's great views

Budapest at night is something else entirely. The Parliament building lit up from the water, Buda Castle glowing gold on the hillside above, the Chain Bridge illuminated against the dark sky — the whole city transforms after sunset into one of the most beautiful and atmospheric urban landscapes in Europe.

We spent one evening simply wandering the Danube promenade on the Pest side — the long riverside walk with views across to the illuminated Buda hillside — and it was one of the most memorable evenings of the trip. No particular destination, no agenda, just walking along the river as the city glowed around us.

The Danube evening cruise was one of the signature Budapest experiences even back then — typically a couple of hours on the water with a drink included, sailing past the Parliament, under the bridges and back, seeing the whole illuminated panorama from the river. The view of the Parliament from the water at night is extraordinary — and the cruise is excellent value.

♨️ The Thermal Baths

Budapest's thermal baths — a 2,000-year-old tradition

Budapest sits on a geological fault line with over 120 natural hot springs beneath the city — a fact that the Romans discovered and the Ottomans developed into the bathing culture that still defines the city today. Visiting at least one of Budapest's thermal baths is not optional — it is the single most uniquely Budapest experience available and something you will find nowhere else in Europe on this scale.

Baths
♨️ Gellért Baths — the grandest in the city

The Gellért Baths are housed in the extraordinary Art Nouveau Gellért Hotel at the foot of Gellért Hill — one of the most beautiful buildings in Budapest — and the baths themselves match the grandeur of the exterior. The main indoor pool is stunning — high vaulted ceilings, ornate tilework, columns and the warm geothermal water filling a space that looks more like a cathedral than a swimming pool. The outdoor terraces and wave pool add to the experience. Going to the Gellért Baths is one of those genuinely special experiences that stays with you for years.

The thermal bath experience in Budapest is about more than just swimming — it is about soaking in water that has been rising through limestone from deep underground for thousands of years, in buildings designed to celebrate that fact, surrounded by locals who have been doing exactly this for generations. It is slower, more meditative and more memorable than any spa anywhere in Western Europe.

🍺 The Ruin Bars

Budapest's ruin bars — nothing like them anywhere in the world

"Random furniture from every decade, graffiti on every surface, old televisions stacked in corners, plants growing through the walls, a courtyard packed with people who looked like they had been there for several days. Welcome to Szimpla Kert. Welcome to Budapest's ruin bar scene. Nothing like it existed anywhere in Western Europe."

Budapest's ruin bars are one of the most genuinely creative and original nightlife concepts in the world — bars built in abandoned buildings and derelict courtyards in the Jewish Quarter of the Pest 7th district, furnished with salvaged furniture, decorated with mismatched art and random objects, and somehow more atmospheric and more enjoyable than any polished cocktail bar in any city you have ever visited.

The scene began in the early 2000s as genuine grassroots creativity — young Hungarians taking spaces that had been abandoned since the communist era and turning them into something completely their own. By 2009, when we visited, it was becoming known across Europe but was still raw and genuinely alternative. Random chairs from different decades. Old bicycles hanging from ceilings. Graffiti covering every surface. A bar stocking every Hungarian spirit you have never heard of. And a courtyard full of people from every country in Europe having the time of their lives.

Szimpla Kert was the most famous even then — the original and still the best. But the whole neighbourhood had bars in similar styles and wandering between them was one of the great Budapest experiences.

🏛️ Heroes' Square & City Park

Heroes' Square, City Park and more of Budapest

Square
🗿 Heroes' Square — a thousand years of Hungarian history

Heroes' Square at the end of Andrássy Avenue is one of the grandest public spaces in Europe — a vast plaza dominated by the Millennium Monument, a 36-metre column topped by the Archangel Gabriel, flanked by colonnades featuring statues of Hungarian chieftains and kings spanning a thousand years of history. It was built to celebrate Hungary's 1,000th anniversary in 1896 and it delivers the grandeur that occasion demanded.

Castle
🏰 Vajdahunyad Castle — the fairytale in the park

Just behind Heroes' Square in City Park, Vajdahunyad Castle is one of Budapest's most charming surprises — a romantic lakeside castle that looks like it was lifted from a Transylvanian valley and placed in a park. It was built for the 1896 millennium exhibition, incorporating architectural styles from different periods of Hungarian history in one extraordinary building. Reflected in the park lake it is genuinely beautiful.

Memorial
👟 Shoes on the Danube — a deeply moving memorial

The Shoes on the Danube Bank is one of the most powerful and moving memorials we have encountered anywhere in Europe — sixty pairs of iron shoes placed on the edge of the Danube bank, marking the spot where Jewish people were forced to remove their shoes before being shot into the river by Arrow Cross militiamen in 1944 and 1945. Simple, quiet and devastating. Stand there for a few minutes and it stays with you for a very long time.

Market
🛒 Central Market Hall — food, paprika and folk art

The Central Market Hall is Budapest's largest and most famous market — a stunning late-19th century building filled with food stalls, vegetable sellers, butchers, spice merchants and, on the upper floor, Hungarian folk art and souvenir stalls. The paprika display alone is worth the visit — every variety and shade of Hungary's most important spice, sold in beautifully designed tins and packets. Brilliant for food shopping, brilliant for atmosphere.

🥣 The Food

Hungarian food — hearty, paprika-rich and brilliantly cheap

Budapest in 2009 was extraordinarily affordable by Irish standards — and the food was not just cheap but genuinely brilliant. Hungarian cuisine is hearty, warming and built around a handful of extraordinary ingredients — paprika above all, but also sour cream, beef, pork and the flavours of Central European cooking that you simply don't find anywhere else.

🥣
Goulash Soup

Hungary's national dish — rich paprika beef soup. Warming, deeply flavoured and completely delicious.

🍗
Chicken Paprikash

Chicken in a creamy paprika sauce, usually served with egg noodles. One of the great Hungarian comfort dishes.

🌀
Chimney Cakes

Kürtőskalács — spiral pastry coated in sugar and cinnamon, cooked over charcoal. Budapest's essential street food.

🍖
Schnitzel

Enormous breaded cutlets — Central European schnitzel culture at its finest, served with pickles and potato.

🍺
Hungarian Beer

Cheap, cold and plentiful. In 2009 a pint cost a fraction of the price at home. Some things have changed less than others.

🌶️
Paprika Everything

Hungary produces the world's finest paprika — it appears in almost everything and the market hall sells it in beautiful tins.

Common questions

Budapest FAQ

Is Budapest worth visiting?
Budapest is one of the great European city breaks — extraordinary grandeur, thermal baths, legendary nightlife and food that is both brilliant and remarkably affordable. The Parliament building alone is worth the trip, and the evening view of Buda Castle and the Chain Bridge reflected in the Danube is one of the most beautiful urban scenes in all of Europe.
What are the best thermal baths in Budapest?
The Gellért Baths are the grandest and most atmospheric — a stunning Art Nouveau building with ornate indoor pools. Széchenyi Baths in City Park are the most famous and most social, with large outdoor pools where locals play chess in the water. Both are brilliant and visiting at least one is an absolutely essential part of any Budapest trip.
What are Budapest ruin bars?
Budapest's ruin bars began appearing in the early 2000s in abandoned buildings and courtyards in the Jewish Quarter — taking derelict spaces and filling them with mismatched furniture, graffiti, old TVs and an atmosphere unlike any bar in Western Europe. Szimpla Kert is the most famous. In 2009 the scene was still raw and genuinely alternative. Today it is more polished but still extraordinary.
Is a Danube river cruise worth doing in Budapest?
Yes — completely. The Parliament building lit up from the water, Buda Castle glowing on the hillside, the Chain Bridge illuminated — it is one of the most beautiful city views in Europe seen from the best possible angle. The evening cruises with a drink included are excellent value and one of the signature Budapest experiences.
What food should you try in Budapest?
Goulash soup is essential — rich, paprika-spiced and deeply delicious. Chicken paprikash is brilliant. Chimney cakes from street stalls are a must. And schnitzel-style dishes are everywhere, enormous and very inexpensive. Hungarian food is hearty, warming and built around some extraordinary flavours that you don't find in Western European cooking.
What is the best way to see Buda Castle?
Walk up via the steep paths from the Chain Bridge for the full sense of the hill and brilliant views — or take the historic Castle Hill Funicular for a fun short ride to the top. Once there, Fisherman's Bastion is just a short walk away and should not be missed. The views from the top over Pest and the Danube are extraordinary at any time of day.
