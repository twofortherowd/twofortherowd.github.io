Sunny Beach, Bulgaria —
the family holiday that ended in total chaos

Our trip to Sunny Beach in 2000 was one of those classic late-90s family holidays that ended in complete and utter chaos. Bulgaria still felt very different from the Spain or Portugal holidays Irish families were used to — cheaper, rougher around the edges and still carrying a visible post-communist atmosphere. We had a week of Black Sea beaches, cheap ice cream, market stalls and a brilliant mountain day trip into rural Bulgaria. And then, on the final night, torrential rain hit, the hotel flooded, Nicola got stuck in the lift, and we ended up wading out with our trousers rolled up carrying bags through waterlogged corridors. Absolute disaster. Completely unforgettable.

🌊 The Story

A Black Sea holiday that turned into family legend

"Some holidays are remembered for sunsets and beautiful beaches. Bulgaria is remembered for soaked shoes, a flooded hotel lobby, panic around the lifts and everyone wading out with trousers rolled up."

At the time, Bulgaria still felt like a very different kind of holiday from the Spain and Portugal trips Irish families were used to. It was cheaper, less polished and more obviously in transition. Sunny Beach was developing quickly, with big hotels, beach bars, markets, nightlife and long sandy beaches along the Black Sea coast.

During the week, it had all the classic ingredients of a family resort holiday: swimming in the Black Sea, eating cheap ice cream and pizza, browsing market stalls full of fake designer gear and souvenirs, walking the busy promenade in the evenings and sitting out in the warm air watching the nightlife come alive.

But Bulgaria also gave us something more memorable than the resort itself: a day trip into the mountains, where we saw a completely different side of the country — villages, traditional crafts, horse carts and people living much more simply than anything we were used to at home.

🏖️ Sunny Beach

Sunny Beach in 2000 — cheap, busy and still rough around the edges

Sunny Beach was already becoming one of Bulgaria's best-known Black Sea resorts, but in 2000 it still felt very different from the slicker Mediterranean destinations. It had a developing-resort feel: big hotels, cheap restaurants, souvenir stalls, beach bars and a nightlife scene that was clearly growing fast.

"It was not polished like Spain. That was the whole point. Bulgaria felt cheaper, more adventurous and slightly unpredictable — which, as it turned out, was very accurate."

🧱 Bulgaria in Transition

The post-communist atmosphere we noticed even as teenagers

Bulgaria in 2000 still felt like a country in transition, only a decade after the fall of communism. Even as teenagers and kids, we could sense that it was different from Western Europe. The resort was geared towards tourists, but beyond it there were visible contrasts everywhere.

"It felt fascinating because it was not like home, and it was not like Spain. Bulgaria still had a rawness to it then — old systems, new tourism, rural poverty, resort development and Black Sea sunshine all sitting side by side."

That was part of what made the trip memorable. Sunny Beach had the beach-holiday surface, but Bulgaria beyond the hotel zone felt older, poorer, more traditional and much less familiar than the holiday destinations we were used to.

🏔️ The Mountain Day Trip

The day trip that showed us rural Bulgaria

One of the highlights of the holiday was a day trip into the mountains, away from the tourist resorts. We travelled through little Bulgarian villages and saw a completely different side of the country: traditional crafts, old stone houses, horse carts and people living far more simply than we were used to in Ireland.

"For us as Irish teenagers and kids, it felt fascinating. This was not a resort excursion in the polished sense — it felt like seeing a country that was still holding onto older ways of life while tourism was arriving fast on the coast."

🌧️ The Final-Night Disaster

The biblical rain, the flooded hotel and Nicola stuck in the lift

The part of the holiday nobody ever forgot was the final night. Out of nowhere, torrential rain hit Sunny Beach. Absolute biblical rain. The hotel started flooding and water began pouring through parts of the building.

"In the middle of all the panic, Nicola got stuck in the lift while the storm was going on — which at the time felt absolutely terrifying."

Eventually everyone had to evacuate through floodwater. We rolled our trousers up and literally waded our way out of the hotel carrying bags and suitcases through waterlogged corridors and flooded streets.

The final memories of Bulgaria were not sunsets or beaches. They were soaked shoes, panic in the hotel lobby, flooded lifts, people scrambling through water and the sheer madness of trying to get out of the hotel during the storm.

Common questions

Bulgaria FAQ

What was Sunny Beach Bulgaria like in 2000?
Sunny Beach in 2000 felt cheaper, rougher around the edges and very different from the Spain and Portugal resorts Irish families were used to. It had long Black Sea beaches, big hotels, beach bars, market stalls, nightlife and a visible post-communist atmosphere as Bulgaria was still in transition.
Is Sunny Beach good for family holidays?
Sunny Beach became popular with families because it had long sandy beaches, large hotels, cheap food, markets, promenades and lots of resort entertainment. Around 2000 it felt more adventurous and less polished than Western European resort destinations.
What can you do on a day trip from Sunny Beach?
Day trips from Sunny Beach can include mountain villages, traditional craft workshops, local artisans, rural Bulgarian communities, Nessebar, Black Sea coastal stops and cultural tours away from the resort area.
What is Bulgaria known for as a travel destination?
Bulgaria is known for Black Sea beaches, mountain villages, traditional crafts, Orthodox monasteries, post-communist history, cheap resort holidays, hearty food and a mix of Balkan, Slavic and Ottoman influences.
Is Bulgaria cheap compared with Spain or Portugal?
In 2000 Bulgaria felt much cheaper than Spain or Portugal for Irish families, especially for food, souvenirs, market goods and resort spending. That value was one of the big reasons Sunny Beach became popular.
