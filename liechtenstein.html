🇱🇮 The Story

The principality that surprised us completely

A day in one of the world's most extraordinary little countries.

"We pulled up expecting a quick stop and ended up completely enchanted. Liechtenstein has this rare quality — a country that is entirely at ease with what it is, utterly confident in its identity, and genuinely stunning in every direction you look."

We came to Liechtenstein in 2016 on a day trip from Germany, not entirely sure what to expect. What we found completely exceeded anything we had anticipated. This tiny Alpine principality — just 160 square kilometres, sandwiched between Switzerland and Austria — is one of the most quietly special places in Europe.

The drive in alone set the tone. The Rhine Valley opens up into a landscape of green hills, forested slopes and mountain peaks that makes you feel like you've stepped into a fairy tale. And then above the capital Vaduz, perched on its rocky hill like something from a storybook, sits Vaduz Castle — the home of the Princely Family of Liechtenstein, still in private use today. You cannot go inside, but the walk up through the vineyards to the castle grounds gives you breathtaking views over the Rhine Valley and into Switzerland.

Vaduz itself is a delightful little capital to wander — small enough to cover on foot but full of interesting shops, cafés, galleries and the National Museum. The Postal Museum was a genuine highlight and far more fascinating than the name might suggest — Liechtenstein's stamp collection is world-famous and the museum does a wonderful job of bringing the country's unique history and identity to life.

The hiking trails that fan out from the valley into the Alpine terrain above are world class — and if you have more time than a day trip allows, Liechtenstein more than rewards a longer stay. It is a place that gets under your skin quietly and leaves you thinking about it long after you have gone.

Common questions

Liechtenstein FAQ

Is Liechtenstein worth visiting?
Absolutely — Liechtenstein is one of Europe's most underrated and overlooked destinations. The Alpine scenery is stunning, the history is fascinating, the capital Vaduz is charming and walkable, and it has a unique atmosphere you simply won't find anywhere else. Most people drive through without stopping — don't make that mistake.
How do you get to Liechtenstein?
Liechtenstein has no airport or railway station, but it is easily reached by bus from nearby Sargans or Buchs in Switzerland, or from Feldkirch in Austria. By road it is very accessible from Switzerland, Austria and Germany — we visited from Germany and it was a straightforward and very enjoyable drive through stunning Alpine scenery.
What is there to do in Liechtenstein?
More than you might expect for such a small country. Vaduz Castle is the iconic landmark perched above the capital — you can't enter but the walk up and the views are worth every step. The Postal Museum is genuinely fascinating. The capital Vaduz is lovely to wander. And the hiking and cycling trails through the Alpine landscape are world class.
Is Liechtenstein a country?
Yes — Liechtenstein is a fully independent principality and one of the world's smallest countries. It is doubly landlocked — surrounded entirely by Switzerland and Austria — and is one of only two doubly landlocked countries in the world. It has its own head of state, its own currency and its own fascinating national identity.
Can you get a Liechtenstein passport stamp?
Liechtenstein is part of the Schengen Area so there is no automatic passport stamp at the border. However, you can get your passport stamped as a souvenir at the Vaduz Tourist Office for a small fee — a popular memento for visitors who want proof they made it to one of Europe's most unusual countries.
How long do you need in Liechtenstein?
A full day is enough to see the highlights — Vaduz Castle, the Postal Museum, a wander around the capital and the Alpine views. If you enjoy hiking or cycling you could easily fill two days. It makes a brilliant day trip from Switzerland, Austria or southern Germany.
