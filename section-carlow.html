Mad facts about Carlow

The stories that make people say "wait, that happened in Carlow?"

A doctor who blew up a medieval castle trying to convert it into a lunatic asylum. A Gothic mansion burned to the ground under suspicious circumstances with a banshee curse attached. And a prehistoric tomb with a capstone heavier than anything else in Europe — sitting in a field three kilometres from the town centre. Carlow, ladies and gentlemen.

💣
The Doctor Who Blew Up Carlow Castle

In 1814, a local physician called Dr Philip Parry Middleton leased Carlow Castle — a magnificent 13th-century Norman fortress that had survived 600 years of war, sieges and Cromwell — with the intention of converting it into a lunatic asylum. To create more space, he decided to use gunpowder to thin the walls. He used rather too much. The two eastern towers and their adjoining walls collapsed completely. Today, only the western wall and two towers remain. One historian of the time described him as "a ninny-pated physician" who used "blasts of gunpowder for enlarging the windows and diminishing the walls." If it had survived intact, Carlow Castle would have been comparable in scale and grandeur to Kilkenny Castle — one of Ireland's top tourist attractions. Instead it's a very photogenic partial ruin, and the rubble from Middleton's explosion actually formed the foundation for much of modern Carlow town centre. At least it was useful for something.

🔥
Duckett's Grove — the Banshee, the Curse and the Mysterious Fire

About ten kilometres from Carlow town sit the extraordinary Gothic ruins of Duckett's Grove — once the centrepiece of a 12,000-acre estate, redesigned in the 1820s into a spectacular castellated Gothic mansion with towers, turrets, statues and grand gardens. In the early 1920s, the abandoned house was used as a base by the local IRA flying column. They left the interior completely untouched — testament, it was said, to the respect the community held for the Duckett family who had treated their tenants well. Then, on the night of 20 April 1933, the house burned to the ground. The cause was never officially established. Local speculation ranged from insurance fraud to tax avoidance — a roofless house did not attract property tax. It has never been resolved. And then there is the curse. A legend holds that a young woman had an affair with one of the Ducketts, died after a fall from her horse on the estate, and her mother placed a Piseóg on the family — an Irish curse intended to bring ruin. A banshee is said to haunt the ruined towers to this day. The ruins are free to visit, the gardens are beautiful and the ghost stories are entirely optional.

🗿
Browne's Hill Dolmen — Older Than the Pyramids, Three Minutes from Town

Three kilometres east of Carlow town, in a field off the R726, sits one of the most extraordinary prehistoric monuments in Europe — and almost nobody outside the county knows it exists. Browne's Hill Dolmen is a Neolithic portal tomb built between 4,000 and 3,300 BC — predating the Egyptian pyramids by over a thousand years. Its granite capstone weighs an estimated 100 to 150 tonnes, making it the heaviest megalithic capstone in Europe. Nobody knows with certainty how the people of Neolithic Ireland lifted a 100-tonne slab of granite onto standing stones. Nobody knows exactly what ceremonies were performed here. The site has never been excavated. It sits in a quiet field with a small car park, a path through the grass and almost no visitors on most days. Free to visit, open always, and genuinely one of the most remarkable things in Ireland.
