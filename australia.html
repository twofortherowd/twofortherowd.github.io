🇦🇺 The Story

What a country. What a contrast. What a trip.

"Australia keeps changing on you. One day you're diving the Great Barrier Reef, the next you're on a catamaran in the Whitsundays, then you're at the Sydney Opera House, then you're driving to Canberra, then you're standing in front of Uluru with nothing but red earth and silence for miles in every direction. It never stops surprising you."

This was part of an around the world trip that Nicola did with friends — flying from Singapore into Perth and eventually making her way home via the Northern Territory, New Zealand, Honolulu and LA. Australia took up a huge chunk of that adventure and delivered something completely new at every single stop.

Perth started the Australian chapter — a couple of nights in the most isolated major city on earth with a private taxi driver who gave them a city tour of Perth and its surrounds. Nice enough but honestly not one of Australia's most immediately characterful cities. The real magic was still to come.

The East Coast tour — Cairns, the reef, Fraser Island, the Whitsundays, Sydney, the road south to Melbourne — was the kind of trip that takes years to properly absorb. And then the Northern Territory, done independently after New Zealand, added an entirely different dimension. Alice Springs, the flying doctors, the saloon bars and three nights at Uluru. One continuous adventure from beginning to end.

We also spent time in Tasmania — which completely caught us off guard and turned out to be one of the most jaw-dropping places on the entire trip. And from Australia we continued the adventure into New Zealand — which is covered in its own page.

🌇 Perth — The Start

Perth — the most isolated city on earth

The Australian adventure began in Perth — flying in from Singapore, the first stop on Australian soil. They hired a private taxi driver who gave them a personal city tour of Perth and the surrounding area, which was a lovely way to get oriented.

Perth is pleasant — clean, sunny, beautifully situated on the Swan River — but it's honest to say it felt a little devoid of character compared to what was coming. It's the most geographically isolated major city on earth, which gives it a certain self-contained quality. Nice to begin there, but the real adventure was ahead.

The Blue Mountains were breathtaking — absolutely out of this world. Driving through them with the vast green valleys dropping away on all sides and the haze hanging in the eucalyptus air is one of those experiences that stays with you.

🐠 The Great Barrier Reef

Diving the Great Barrier Reef — one of the best things we've ever done

"The world underneath the ocean is something to be reckoned with. I did two dives and it was just — wow. The colour, the fish, the coral, the sheer scale of it all. No photograph captures it. You have to get in the water."

The Great Barrier Reef is one of those bucket list experiences that people sometimes worry will disappoint — too hyped, too touristy. It does not disappoint. Nicola did two dives and rates it as one of her absolute favourite experiences of the entire around the world trip. The reef stretches over 2,300km along the Queensland coast and diving it is genuinely like entering another world.

The colour of the coral, the variety of the fish, the clarity of the water and the sheer otherness of spending time beneath the ocean's surface — it is extraordinary. Even for an inexperienced diver, the guided dive experience is accessible and absolutely worthwhile. Do not settle for just snorkelling if you can dive.

🌊 The East Coast

Fraser Island, the Whitsundays and the East Coast tour

Fraser Island was gorgeous — the world's largest sand island, with freshwater lakes sitting incongruously in the middle of sand dunes, dingoes roaming the beaches and a landscape that looks like it shouldn't exist but absolutely does. A brilliant stop that often gets overlooked by people racing between Cairns and Sydney.

The Whitsundays were everything they promised. Out on a catamaran in the middle of some of the most beautiful water in the world — the Coral Sea, dotted with islands, the impossibly white sand of Whitehaven Beach in the distance. One of those days where you keep stopping to make sure what you're seeing is actually real.

Byron Bay brought the relaxed East Coast energy — easy-going, sunny and the kind of place that immediately makes you want to slow down. One of those places you can understand why people go for a week and end up staying for a month.

And then there was the overnight stay on a ranch — one of those unexpected tour stops that turns into a brilliant memory. Horses, red dust, a night under the stars somewhere out in the Australian countryside. Class craic.

🌉 Sydney

Sydney — sunshine, the Opera House and two trips to Palm Beach

Sydney is everything it's supposed to be — the Opera House is as magnificent in person as in every photograph, the harbour is stunning and the sunshine is relentless. The buzz of arriving in Sydney for the first time is something genuinely special.

Going to see an opera at the Sydney Opera House was a bucket list moment done properly — inside the building is as extraordinary as outside it, and the experience of being in one of the world's most iconic buildings for a performance is something that stays with you.

📺 The Palm Beach situation: Went to Palm Beach day one — missed the Home and Away cast. Went back day two. Saw the cast. Saw Brax specifically. Was absolutely delighted. Zero regrets about going twice. This is what travel is about. 😄

Beyond the Home and Away pilgrimage, Sydney gave brilliant beach time at Bondi, the harbour walk, great food and that easy Australian warmth that makes the city feel immediately welcoming to Irish visitors.

🏛️ Canberra, the Twelve Apostles & Melbourne

The road south — Canberra, the Great Ocean Road and Melbourne

After Sydney, they rented a car and drove south — stopping in Canberra for a day. Australia's purpose-built capital is genuinely interesting — the parliament buildings, the National War Memorial, the beautiful lake at the heart of the planned city. Worth a day if you're driving between Sydney and Melbourne.

Then came one of the great moments of the entire trip — a helicopter flight over the Twelve Apostles on the Great Ocean Road. The limestone stacks rising from the Southern Ocean are spectacular from the cliff tops — from the air they are extraordinary. One of those upgrades that is absolutely worth every cent.

Melbourne was a revelation. Lots of Irish people, a brilliant food and café scene, beautiful architecture and a character completely different to Sydney. The highlight was Phillip Island's little penguins — watching the tiny fairy penguins waddle up the beach at dusk in their hundreds is one of those genuinely magical natural experiences that stays with you for years.

🪨 Uluru & Alice Springs

The Northern Territory — a completely different Australia

"After all the coastline and the cities, heading into the Red Centre felt like stepping into a different country entirely. The silence. The red earth. The scale of the sky. And then Uluru — which is simply one of the most extraordinary places on earth."

After New Zealand, Nicola flew independently into Alice Springs to do a tour of the Northern Territory — and this part of the trip turned out to be just as extraordinary as anything that had come before it.

Alice Springs is completely unlike the rest of Australia. A frontier town in the middle of the Outback, surrounded by the MacDonnell Ranges, with a distinct character all of its own. The Royal Flying Doctor Service museum was genuinely fascinating — the story of how medical care reaches the most remote communities in one of the world's most sparsely populated landscapes is extraordinary. And the saloon bars were absolutely brilliant craic — proper Outback pub culture that felt like something out of a film.

Uluru — three nights, which made all the difference. Uluru has a presence and a power that simply cannot be captured in photographs. It is not just the scale of it — though the scale is extraordinary — it is the feeling that surrounds it. Walking the base of the rock at different times of day, watching the colour change from red to gold to purple as the sun moves, understanding a little of what this place means to the Anangu people — it leaves a mark on you that does not fade.

Some places are famous for a reason. This is one of them.

Common questions

Australia FAQ

Is the Great Barrier Reef worth diving?
Absolutely — diving the Great Barrier Reef is one of the great travel experiences on earth. Nicola did two dives and it remains one of her favourite experiences of the entire trip. The colour, the life, the sheer scale of the reef — no snorkel or glass-bottom boat comes close to diving it properly.
Is Uluru worth the long journey?
Yes — without question. Uluru has a presence and a power that is impossible to fully prepare for. Spending three nights there gives you time to really experience it — watching the light change at different times of day, understanding the Aboriginal significance of the place and feeling the extraordinary silence of the Red Centre. Do not do just a day trip if you can avoid it.
Is Palm Beach worth visiting for Home and Away fans?
Absolutely — and go twice if you miss the cast on the first visit, like Nicola did! Palm Beach is a beautiful spot in its own right but for anyone who grew up watching Home and Away it is a proper pilgrimage moment. We can confirm Brax was there on the second visit. 😄
Is Alice Springs worth visiting?
Yes — Alice Springs is completely unlike the rest of Australia. Out in the Red Centre with a distinct frontier town character all of its own. The Royal Flying Doctor Service museum is genuinely fascinating, the saloon bars are brilliant fun and the landscape around the town is extraordinary.
Is Melbourne worth visiting?
Melbourne is one of Australia's most liveable and culturally rich cities — great food, a brilliant bar and coffee scene, a large and warm Irish community and the famous little penguins at Phillip Island nearby. It is a very different vibe to Sydney and well worth spending a few nights.
Is the Twelve Apostles helicopter worth it?
Yes — seeing the Twelve Apostles from the air is one of those upgrades that is absolutely worth every cent. The rock stacks rising from the Southern Ocean are spectacular from the cliff tops but from a helicopter they are extraordinary. Book it.
